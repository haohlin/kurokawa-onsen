Overview

One Village, One Ryokan

Nestled in a mountain gorge in Kumamoto Prefecture, Kurokawa Onsen embodies a rare philosophy: the entire village operates as a single ryokan. Thirty inns share their waters, creating unified hospitality.

Free of concrete hotels and neon signs, the townscape is defined by wooden buildings, earthen walls, stone stairs, and the Tanoharu River. Recognized in the 2009 Michelin Green Guide.

300+ years of history — from Edo-period rest stop for feudal lords to one of Japan's most beloved onsen destinations.

Etiquette

Bathing Manners

Centuries-old customs. Respect these and you'll be welcomed warmly.

1Undress completely — No swimsuits, clothing, or jewelry. Leave everything in the changing room.
2Wash thoroughly first — Sit on the small stools. Rinse completely before entering the bath.
3Keep your towel dry — Never let it touch the water. Balance it on your head or set aside.
4Enter slowly, stay quiet — No splashing, no loud voices. The onsen is a place of calm.
5Tattoo policy — Many ryokan restrict tattoos. Check ahead — some offer cover-ups or private baths.
Essentials

Practical Tips

What to Bring

●Cash — No ATMs in town. Bring sufficient yen.
●Small towel — Useful for spontaneous bath-hopping.
●Pocket WiFi — Limited connectivity. Embrace the disconnect.
●Warm layers — For strolling between baths in cold air.

Good to Know

●Dine early — Restaurants close by 6 PM. Ryokan dinner is the main event.
●Start baths at noon — Before check-ins for quieter soaking.
●English maps at the info center.
●No convenience stores — Unplugged. Part of the charm.
